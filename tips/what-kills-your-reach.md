# What Kills Your Reach on X: A Diagnostic Guide

Based on the open-source X recommendation algorithm source code. Every mechanism cited here maps to actual filter classes, scoring weights, or pipeline stages in the codebase.

---

## 1. TL;DR — The Most Important Things That Kill Reach

In order of impact:

1. **Not-dwelled scrolls** — users scrolling past without reading. This is a passive, negative-weight signal that fires constantly and silently.
2. **Net-negative score** — once your predicted mutes/reports outweigh predicted likes, you fall below a hard offset floor and rank below every positive-scoring post.
3. **Hard social graph filters** — mutes and blocks remove you before scoring even begins. You are invisible to those users, not just de-ranked.
4. **Grox safety classification** — offline classifiers label your post before it is ever served. A safety flag means `VFFilter` removes your post from every ranking batch.
5. **Author diversity decay** — posting multiple times in rapid succession means your 3rd and 4th posts score at roughly 33% and 21% respectively.
6. **Muted keywords** — token-based matching on viewer mute lists removes posts before scoring.
7. **Age and seen-post filters** — stale posts and already-served posts are hard-filtered regardless of quality.

---

## 2. Negative Scoring Signals — Ranked by How Commonly They're Triggered

The ranking score formula is:

```
score = Σ(weight_i × P(action_i))
```

Each action has a weight. Negative-weight actions subtract from your score. The actions below have **negative weights** and directly reduce your ranking score.

| Signal | Mechanism | How Often It Fires |
|---|---|---|
| `not_dwelled_score` | Viewer scrolls past without dwelling | Constantly — every impression that doesn't pause |
| `mute_author_score` | Viewer has muted you | Per muted follower, every request |
| `report_score` | Viewer reports the post | Rare, but high negative weight |
| `not_interested_score` | Viewer taps "Not interested" | Moderate — requires explicit action |
| `block_author_score` | Predicted probability viewer would block you | Rare trigger; a separate hard filter (`AuthorSocialgraphFilter`) handles actual existing blocks before scoring |

**Key insight:** `not_dwelled_score` is the only signal that fires without any user intent. Every impression that doesn't result in a pause, like, reply, or click contributes a negative weight. Post to an uninterested audience repeatedly and your score degrades on every impression.

---

## 3. `not_dwelled` — The Silent Reach Killer

Most creators focus on likes and replies. The algorithm also tracks the **absence of engagement**.

`not_dwelled_score` fires when a user's feed loads your post and they scroll past it without pausing. This is not something users consciously do — it is a passive signal generated by every non-engaged impression.

**Why this is dangerous:**

- If your post appears in the feeds of users who have no interest in it (e.g., via aggressive hashtag use, trending topic injection, or OON discovery mismatches), every one of those appearances generates a `not_dwelled` negative signal.
- A post shown to 10,000 uninterested users accumulates 10,000 negative signals before a single "Not interested" tap is ever needed.
- This is the primary mechanism by which poorly-targeted viral attempts backfire. The algorithm interprets broad exposure with low dwell as evidence the post is poor quality.

**What triggers dwell vs. not-dwell:** The model predicts `P(dwell)` based on historical behavior. A user who typically reads long threads but scrolls past yours contributes a stronger negative signal than a user who always scrolls fast.

**Mitigation:** Post to an audience that is actually interested. OON discovery (PhoenixSource) selects candidates based on Grox embeddings matching viewer interests — if your topic embeddings don't match, your post appears to uninterested users and accumulates `not_dwelled` negatives.

## 4. Hard Filters — Removed Before Scoring Even Starts

Sections 4.1–4.5 are **pre-scoring** filters that run before any ranking score is computed. Section 4.6 (`VFFilter`) runs **post-selection** — after scoring and top-K truncation. Section 4.7–4.8 are pre-scoring. A post removed by any filter here contributes zero visible impressions for that viewer.

### 4.1 `AuthorSocialgraphFilter`

Removes posts based on the social graph relationship between viewer and author. Conditions that trigger removal:

- Viewer has **muted** you
- Viewer has **blocked** you
- **You have blocked** the viewer (bidirectional — you are invisible to people you blocked)
- The quoted author has blocked the viewer
- Viewer has **blocked the quoted author**
- Viewer has blocked the retweeted user

This is the hardest filter. It is unconditional and runs first. No amount of engagement signals overrides a block or mute.

### 4.2 `MutedKeywordFilter`

Removes posts whose text matches any keyword the viewer has muted. The matching is **token-based**, not substring — `"crypto"` in a mute list matches the token `crypto` but not `cryptocurrency` (unless that token is also muted separately). Posts are removed before scoring.

### 4.3 `AgeFilter`

Posts older than `max_age` are dropped. Age is derived from the Snowflake ID embedded in the tweet ID. The cutoff is configurable per pipeline but applies universally. Old posts do not get a second chance via algorithmic surfacing — they are hard-filtered.

### 4.4 `PreviouslySeenPostsFilter`

Uses a **bloom filter** plus exact ID matching to remove posts the viewer has already seen. If a post was shown in a previous session and logged, it is excluded. This filter is approximate on the bloom filter side — false positives can occasionally suppress unseen posts, but false negatives are rare.

### 4.5 `PreviouslyServedPostsFilter`

Server-tracked list of post IDs already served in the **current session**. Exact match, no approximation. Prevents duplicate serving within a single feed load cycle.

### 4.6 `VFFilter` (post-**selection** removal — runs after scoring AND top-K selection)

After scoring and top-K selection, posts flagged by the **visibility filter system** are removed. Safety labels are pre-computed offline by Grox and hydrated at serving time. If a label marks the post for dropping (`visibility_reason` with `Action::Drop`), `VFFilter` removes it regardless of its ranking score. Posts with no visibility reason (`None`) are kept. A post can score highly, make it into the top-K, and still be removed here.

### 4.7 `AncillaryVFFilter`

Grox can set `drop_ancillary_posts = true` on a post. This filter removes any post with that flag. Typically used for posts that are technically within policy but are contextually inappropriate for recommendation surfaces.

### 4.8 `IneligibleSubscriptionFilter`

Subscription-gated posts are removed if the viewer is not a subscriber of the author. This is a hard eligibility gate, not a ranking factor.

## 5. Grox Safety — How Offline Classification Gates Your Reach

Grox is X's offline ML safety and content classification pipeline. It runs **before** posts are ever served — the results are precomputed and stored.

### The pipeline:

1. **`PlanPostSafety`** — evaluates the post for safety policy violations
2. **`PlanSafetyPtos`** — PTOS (policy classification) labels the post for specific policy categories
3. Labels are stored offline and hydrated during candidate selection
4. **`VFFilter`** reads these labels at serving time and drops flagged posts

### Key behaviors:

- **Default brand safety is `MediumRisk`** — posts that fail Grox hydration (Grox didn't return labels) are not treated as safe by default. They are treated as `MediumRisk`. This means Grox failures can suppress posts even when no actual policy violation occurred.
- **Repeated safety flags escalate** — multiple flagged posts from the same account contribute to account-level suppression signals, not just post-level removal.
- **No serving-time appeal** — because labels are precomputed offline, a post that gets flagged cannot be "rescued" by high engagement at serving time. The label wins.

### What triggers Grox flags:

- Policy-violating content (explicit, hateful, spam patterns)
- Coordinated inauthentic behavior signals
- High report rates from prior posts on the account
- Text patterns that match known spam templates

### What you cannot see:

Grox labels are not exposed to the author. You will not receive a notification that your post was flagged — it simply stops appearing in recommendation feeds. The only observable signal is a sudden drop in impressions from non-followers.

## 6. The Offset Normalization Cliff — Net-Negative Means Ranked Below Everything

This is the structural feature that makes recovery from a bad streak difficult.

### How scores are bucketed:

- **Net-positive posts** (predicted likes outweigh predicted mutes/reports) occupy the range `[NEGATIVE_SCORES_OFFSET, ∞)`
- **Net-negative posts** (predicted mutes/reports outweigh predicted likes) occupy the range `[0, NEGATIVE_SCORES_OFFSET)`

A net-negative post **always ranks below every net-positive post**, regardless of absolute scores. A net-negative post with a raw score of 9,999 ranks below a net-positive post with a raw score of 0.001.

### Why this matters:

Once a post crosses into net-negative territory, it cannot compete with any positively-scoring content for the same viewer. The offset is a hard cliff, not a gradual slope.

### What pushes a post net-negative:

- High `P(mute)` or `P(report)` predictions from the model
- High `not_dwelled` probability for this author's content with this viewer segment
- Prior account-level signals that shift Bayesian priors toward negative outcomes

### Recovery:

Net-negative scores are per-post and per-viewer-segment. A post that is net-negative for one segment may be net-positive for a different audience. However, once a post has accumulated enough negative signals in the live system, the model's posterior for that post shifts — there is no mechanism to "reset" a post's score history.

## 7. Structural Suppression: Author Diversity Decay

The ranking pipeline applies an **author diversity penalty** when the same author appears multiple times in a scored batch.

### The decay schedule:

| Post # in batch | Score multiplier |
|---|---|
| 1st | 100% |
| 2nd | ~55% |
| 3rd | ~33% |
| 4th | ~21% |

This is exponential decay. Each subsequent post from the same author in the same ranking batch receives a progressively lower effective score.

### Implications:

- Posting 4 times in rapid succession means your 4th post competes at 21% of its natural score against posts from other authors at 100%.
- During high-volume posting windows (e.g., live-tweeting an event), most of your posts will be heavily penalized even if individually high quality.
- The decay applies per-batch, so timing posts across separate feed-load cycles reduces the penalty.

### What "batch" means:

A batch corresponds to a single candidate retrieval + ranking cycle, which is tied to a viewer's feed request. A viewer who refreshes their feed triggers a new batch. If your posts were served and filtered in the previous batch, the decay does not carry over to the next request.

## 8. What To Do If Your Reach Suddenly Drops — Diagnostic Checklist

Use this checklist in order. Each step maps to a specific algorithm mechanism.

### Step 1: Check for Grox safety flag (VFFilter)
- Are impressions from **non-followers** specifically dropping while follower impressions remain stable?
- If yes: OON discovery is suppressed. Likely cause is a Grox safety label on recent posts.
- Action: Review recent posts for policy-adjacent content. Check if any recent post received an unusual report volume.

### Step 2: Check for net-negative score (offset cliff)
- Is engagement rate (likes/impressions) also dropping, not just raw impressions?
- If yes: The post may have crossed net-negative. This happens when `not_dwelled` signals accumulate faster than likes.
- Action: Examine recent posts that had broad distribution but low engagement. These accumulated `not_dwelled` negatives.

### Step 3: Check for author diversity decay
- Did you post more than twice in a short window?
- Action: Check your posting frequency. If you posted 3–4+ times in an hour, expect significant decay on posts 3 and 4.

### Step 4: Check muted keyword exposure
- Did recent posts use terms that are commonly muted? (finance spam terms, political hot-button phrases, certain hashtags)
- `MutedKeywordFilter` is viewer-specific — you won't see it directly, but posts with commonly-muted terms reduce effective reach across a segment of your audience.

### Step 5: Check OON discovery eligibility
- PhoenixSource (OON/For You discovery) is **disabled** for: Ranked Following tab requests, cached mode, single-topic feed requests (≤6 topics), and when the new-user topic retrieval path is active. Note: `NewUserTopicIdsFilter` restricts which OON posts reach new users — PhoenixSource may still run but its outputs are filtered by topic.
- If your account recently had Grox embedding failures, your post may not appear in OON candidate pools at all.
- Action: Check if reach drop is isolated to the For You tab vs. Following tab. If Following tab is stable, the issue is in OON candidate selection.

### Step 6: Check social graph filters
- Did you block a significant number of accounts recently? You are invisible to accounts you block (`AuthorSocialgraphFilter` is bidirectional).
- Are there any accounts with large followings that have muted or blocked you? Their follower overlap will not see your posts.

### Step 7: Check post age
- `AgeFilter` hard-drops posts beyond `max_age`. Amplifying an old post via replies does not resurface it in algorithmic feeds — the post is filtered before scoring regardless of new engagement.
